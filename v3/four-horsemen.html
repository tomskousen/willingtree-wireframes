Dr. John Gottman's warning signs for relationships

What Are the Four Horsemen?

Dr. John Gottman, renowned relationship researcher, identified four communication patterns that predict relationship failure with over 90% accuracy. He calls them the "Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse" because of their destructive power.

These patterns are not occasional slip-ups‚Äîthey're habits that, if left unchecked, will destroy intimacy and connection.

The Four Horsemen

1. Criticism

Attacking your partner's character or personality, rather than addressing a specific behavior.

Example: "You never think about anyone but yourself. You're so selfish."

Antidote: Gentle Start-Up

Express a complaint without blame. Use "I feel..." statements and describe what you need positively.

Example: "I feel frustrated when plans change without notice. Can we check in with each other before making commitments?"

2. Contempt

Treating your partner with disrespect, mockery, ridicule, or sarcasm. Contempt is the single greatest predictor of divorce.

Example: Eye-rolling, name-calling, sneering, hostile humor, or saying "You're pathetic."

Antidote: Build a Culture of Appreciation

Express appreciation and admiration regularly. Remind yourself of your partner's positive qualities, especially during conflict.

Example: "I really appreciate how hard you've been working. I know you're trying."

3. Defensiveness

Refusing to take responsibility and instead making excuses, cross-complaining, or playing the victim.

Example: "It's not my fault‚Äîyou're the one who started this!" or "I wouldn't have done that if you had just..."

Antidote: Take Responsibility

Accept your partner's perspective, even if only partially. Take responsibility for even a small part of the conflict.

Example: "You're right, I should have called. That was inconsiderate. I'm sorry."

4. Stonewalling

Withdrawing from the conversation, shutting down, or giving your partner the silent treatment. Often happens when you're physiologically flooded (overwhelmed).

Example: Turning away, acting busy, not responding, or walking out without explanation.

Antidote: Physiological Self-Soothing

Take a break when you're overwhelmed. Tell your partner you need 20 minutes to calm down, then return to the conversation.

Example: "I'm feeling overwhelmed right now. Can we take a 20-minute break and come back to this? I want to hear you."

Why This Matters

The Four Horsemen don't appear overnight. They develop gradually as couples fail to address small hurts and resentments. Once these patterns become habitual, they create a negative cycle that's hard to break.

Good news: Recognizing these patterns is the first step toward change. By using the antidotes consistently, couples can rebuild trust, respect, and connection.
