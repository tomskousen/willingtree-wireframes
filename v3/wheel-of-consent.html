What is the Wheel of Consent?

The Wheel of Consent is a powerful framework developed by Dr. Betty Martin that helps us understand touch, consent, and mutual pleasure in relationships. At its core, it distinguishes between two fundamental questions:

1. "What do I WANT?" ‚Äî Acting for your own pleasure/benefit
2. "What am I WILLING to do?" ‚Äî Acting for your partner's pleasure/benefit

The Four Quadrants

üéØ TAKING (You Do, For You)

Wanting vs. Willing: The Key Distinction

üíö Wanting (For You)

When you're wanting, you're focused on your own experience, pleasure, or benefit. You're in charge of what happens. This isn't selfish‚Äîit's essential! Knowing what you want and being able to ask for it is a foundational skill in healthy relationships.

Examples: "I want to kiss you," "I want you to listen to me," "I want affection right now."

ü§ù Willing (For Them)

When you're willing, you're offering to do something for your partner's benefit. You accept their desires and choose to participate. Being willing doesn't mean being resentful or coerced‚Äîit means genuine, generous availability without expectation.

Examples: "I'm willing to give you a massage," "I'm willing to listen," "I'm willing to try that activity with you."

Doing vs. Being Done For

üèÉ Doing (You Act)

When you're doing, you're the active participant. You're the one initiating, touching, speaking, or acting. The question is: Are you doing it for yourself (wanting) or for them (willing)?

üßò Being Done For (They Act)

When you're being done for, you're the recipient. You're allowing someone else to touch you, serve you, or act toward you. The question is: Are they doing it for you (accepting their service) or for themselves (allowing their desire)?

Avoiding the Traps

‚ùå The Obligation Trap

Saying you're willing when you're actually resentful creates a toxic dynamic. True willingness is freely given, with no strings attached.

‚ùå The Codependency Trap

Only serving (willing) without ever wanting creates burnout and resentment. Healthy relationships have both giving and receiving.

‚ùå The Selfish Trap

Only taking what you want without considering what you're willing to give creates imbalance. Mutuality requires both wanting and willing.
